# Hunch settling (the executable threshold)

A reusable technique for telling a buildable reaction from a hunch - and for
converging the hunch into a buildable direction WITHOUT burning a build round
on a guess. Any flow that turns user reactions into builder briefs (a mockup
round, a tweak attempt, a revision loop) can run this gate.

The throw/pull craft this technique distills lives canonically in
`agents/riff.md` (Section 3 and the Operating notes) - that file is the
canonical home; this reference adapts the move for main-loop use between
build rounds and never replaces it.

## The executable threshold

The test, run silently on every reaction:
**can I write the one-line alchemist brief using only the user's words?**
A brief needs a target (which element or quality)
and a direction (what it changes toward).
If you would have to supply either noun yourself, the reaction is below the
executable threshold - that gap is the hunch, and filling it silently with
your own hypothesis is the bug this gate exists to kill.

The psychology: taste fires faster than articulation. The verdict in a reaction
("close," "off," "too much") is reliable data - the felt sense is real. The
attribution often isn't there yet, and the user's first guess at their own
reaction can be wrong ("maybe the color?" is frequently a guess at a feeling,
not a diagnosis). Treating a verdict as a spec spends a whole build round
testing YOUR hypothesis about THEIR feeling - and a wrong revision can bury the
exact thing that was pulling them.

## The five below-threshold signals

1. **Verdict without target** - emotion words with no noun a selector could
   attach to: "off," "almost," "doesn't feel like us."
2. **Attribution hedges** - "maybe the spacing?", "I think it's the..." - a
   named target held as a guess is still a hunch.
3. **Conflicting pulls** - "I love A's energy but B's calm" - two attractors,
   no stated resolution; the hybrid isn't specified until they say which
   quality survives where.
4. **Comparative without dimension** - "B over A" with no axis named.
5. **Energy drop** - replies shortening, "sure, I guess" - taste disengaging,
   which usually means the direction is wrong at a level they can't name yet.
   Watch for it hardest late in a loop, where it mimics acceptance-fatigue but
   is not acceptance.

## Above the threshold - do nothing

These proceed full blast, exactly as if this reference didn't exist:

- **Target + direction in the user's words** - "header's too heavy, lighten it."
- **A named donor** - "B with C's cards."
- **Explicit delegation** - "just try something," "surprise me." This is a
  licensed full blast: the user handing you the hunch on purpose is not a gap
  to settle.

Over-triggering on clear reactions is the failure mode of this gate. A reaction
that passes the threshold never gets settled, slowed, or second-guessed.

## The settle move (below threshold)

Both obvious moves are wrong:

- **Build on a guess** - today's failure; the whole round tests your hypothesis.
- **Interrogate** - "what exactly feels off?" asks the user to do the
  articulation they just demonstrated they can't do yet.

The right move is riff's throw/pull, run conversationally in the main loop:

**Throw a concrete interpretation and let them correct it.** "I think it's
reading corporate, not dense - the gray is doing it."
People correct far better than they generate:
"no, not corporate... it's that nothing has weight" is a more articulate
reaction than anything an open question would have produced. Two or three throws usually crosses the threshold - the moment you
can write the one-line brief in their words, stop settling and go build.

In a hunch, below the executable threshold, riffing is the good move - not a
detour from the flow but the flow converging. That is the point of this gate:
the pause IS the progress.

If throw/pull won't settle it - the hunch keeps widening instead of
sharpening - say so plainly and suggest stepping out (park the work, or riff
separately via /craft:riff). Never pretend a direction settled when it didn't.

## Also useful for

This is a general technique, not a mockup-internal detail. Any reaction-driven
revision loop has the same failure mode - the tweak attempt loop is the known
next consumer. A flow running this gate points here rather than re-documenting
the mechanic, and Reads this file once per session, holding the technique
across subsequent reactions.
